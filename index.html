I am an NSF Mathematical Sciences Postdoctoral Fellow in the Department of Applied Mathematics and Statistics at the Colorado School of Mines. My research focuses on space and space-time data, Bayesian methods, and improving statistical methods used in climate science.

Previsiously, I completed my Ph.D. in the Department of Statistics at the University of Washington where I was advised by Adrian Raftery and Cecilia Bitz. My dissertation was on Space-Time Contour Models for Sea Ice Forecasting. Prior to UW, I completed a master's in the Statistics Department at Harvard under the direction of Luke Bornn. My research looked at the biases introduced from using gridded data as a proxy for point-level climate data. I have also worked in the Statistical Sciences Group at Los Alamos National Laboratory.

Recent News

  • July 2020: New preprint posted: Contour Models for Boundaries Enclosing Star-Shaped and Approximately Star-Shaped Polygons. This paper develops the Gaussian Star-shaped Contour Model (GSCM) for inferring distributions from multiple fully-observed contours.

  • March 2020: I was awarded an NSF Mathematical Sciences Postdoctoral Research Fellowship. My project on "Spatiotemporal Methods for Integrating Stochastic Components in Dynamic Sea Ice Models" will develop data-driven methods for implementing stochastic parameterization.

  • March 2020: Our research estimating the effects of shipping pollution on cloud properties was published as an Editors' Highlight paper in AGU Advances and covered by Wired, UW News, and the American Geophysical Union.
